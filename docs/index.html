One morning near the end of October not long before the first drops of the mercilessly long autumn rains began to fall on the cracked and saline soil on the western side of the estate, Futaki woke to hear bells. He propped himself on his elbows on the pillow so as to look out of the mousehole-sized kitchen window that was partly misted up, and directed his gaze to the faint blue dawn sky but the field was still and silent, bathed only in the now ever fainter bell sound, and the only light to be seen was the one glimmering in the doctor's window whose house was set well apart from the others on the far side.
. . .
He gazed sadly at the threatening sky, at the burned-out remnants of a locust-plagued summer, and suddenly saw on the twig of an acacia, as in a vision, the progress of spring, summer, fall and winter, as if the whole of time were a frivolous interlude in the much greater spaces of eternity, a brilliant conjuring trick to produce something apparently orderly out of chaos, to establish a vantage point from which chance might begin to look like necessity.

And he saw himself nailed to the cross of his own cradle and coffin, painfully trying to tear his body away, only, eventually, to deliver himself — utterly naked, without identifying mark, stripped down to essentials — into the care of the people whose duty it was to wash the corpses.

The clock above their heads shows a quarter before ten but what else should they be waiting for? They know what the neon light with its piercing buzz is doing on that ceiling with its hairline cracks and what the timeless echo of those slamming doors is all about; they know why those heavy boots with their half-moon metaled heels are clattering down those strangely high, tiled corridors.

He was surrounded by an utterly silent, utterly still landscape, and he felt as though he were sitting in the most distant spot in the cosmos, far from everything, because there was nothing around him as far as the eye could see, nothing but the faint, undulating line of the horizon, the raw, dark, lowering sky, and the estate.

The spider had spun its web over everything: the glasses, the tables, the window handles, the lamp, and the sleeping faces. It was as if the whole world were slowly being knitted together by patient, tireless hands working in the darkness.

There she was inside the old pigeon loft in the attic, in that single domain whose secrets were known to her alone, where she had no need to fear her elder brother's sudden inexplicable assaults. From that time on, her days were filled with cramping anxiety and she hid herself behind the barn or sometimes at the end of the house.

What is behind me still remains ahead of me. Can't a man rest?

My friends! I confess, I come to you at a difficult time. I am shaken, in other words, I am cast down. Believe me, I too am utterly confused, so you must forgive me if, for now, I cannot find quite the right words.

They have their hoods and hats pulled tight against the driving rain and wind, but their faces are animated with wild, desperate joy. They are looking back at the dark, decaying ruins of a rural estate fading away in the foggy distance.

The atmosphere was completely silent and dreamlike, as if blending horror with mystical awe, and the three of them crouched terrified behind the thick bush, unable to move, unable to look away.

They sat miserably on their cheap suitcases and bundles, shivering in the cold draft, looking utterly defeated and betrayed. The grey light of dawn spilled through large, empty window frames.

The room felt lifeless, stale, and completely divorced from the outside world. The two of them sat across from each other, one slumped in front of the typewriter, the other holding a piece of crumpled paper covered in chaotic, scratchy handwriting, looking deeply exasperated.

He put the glass back in its place, quickly adjusted the matchbox that had slipped off the cigarette pack, then ran his eye over the battle order of demijohns behind the armchair with a certain satisfaction and decided that he was now ready to face the approach of winter.

He put on his glasses and leaned forward, his pencil hovering, then began to write: “One morning near the end of October not long before the first drops of the mercilessly long autumn rains began to fall on the cracked and saline soil on the western side of the estate, Futaki woke to hear bells.
